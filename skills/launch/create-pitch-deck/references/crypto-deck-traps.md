# Crypto Deck Traps

Specific ways crypto pitch decks fail that non-crypto decks don't face. Avoid these.

## Trap 1: Leading with Tokenomics

**What it looks like:** Slide 2 is a token distribution pie chart.

**Why it fails:** Investors hear "token" and think "fundraising disguised as product." It signals you're more interested in raising than building.

**Fix:** Put tokenomics in the appendix. If the product is compelling, they'll ask about the token.

## Trap 2: Governance Theater

**What it looks like:** "Community-governed protocol" with a DAO structure on the deck.

**Why it fails:** Most DAOs are governance in name only. VCs know this. Claiming governance before you have a community is a red flag.

**Fix:** Be honest about who controls the protocol today. Plan for decentralization as a roadmap item, not a current feature.

## Trap 3: "TVL by Q4"

**What it looks like:** A projection that you'll have $100M TVL in 12 months.

**Why it fails:** TVL projections are almost always wrong. They ignore competition, market conditions, and the fact that most of a protocol's TVL comes from incentives, not organic demand.

**Fix:** Show a path to users, not TVL. Users → activity → TVL is the honest sequence.

## Trap 4: Vague Tech Claims

**What it looks like:** "AI-powered, ZK-verified, cross-chain interoperability protocol."

**Why it fails:** Three buzzwords, zero specificity. The reader can't evaluate any of it.

**Fix:** Pick one thing and explain it. "We use a ZK circuit to verify that a user's credit score is above a threshold, without revealing the score." One mechanism, clearly explained.

## Trap 5: The "X for Crypto" Slide

**What it looks like:** "We're the Stripe for DeFi" or "the Robinhood for NFTs."

**Why it fails:** It's lazy. It tells the reader nothing about what you actually do.

**Fix:** Describe the product in plain English. "We let developers accept crypto payments via a single API call."

## Trap 6: Over-Engineering the Architecture

**What it looks like:** A slide with 15 boxes, 8 arrows, and a legend.

**Why it fails:** No one can read it. If you can't explain your architecture in 4 boxes, you don't understand it well enough.

**Fix:** One flow per slide. User → contract → result. Add complexity only if the audience asks.

## Trap 7: The Unverified Team Slide

**What it looks like:** Photos and names with no prior work, no GitHub, no shipped product.

**Why it fails:** In crypto, pseudonymity is common but anonymity without a track record is a risk signal.

**Fix:** If pseudonymous, show the work: GitHub repos, deployed contract addresses, prior hackathon results, community contributions. If public, show relevant prior experience.

## Trap 8: Ignoring the Regulatory Question

**What it looks like:** A token with no mention of securities law or compliance.

**Why it fails:** Every serious investor thinks about this. If you haven't, it signals inexperience.

**Fix:** Address it briefly. "We've consulted with [law firm] on token structure. The token is a utility token with [specific characteristics] that distinguish it from a security under [framework]."

## Trap 9: The "We'll Be on Every Chain" Slide

**What it looks like:** Logos of 12 chains with "multi-chain" as a selling point.

**Why it fails:** Multi-chain is a roadmap item, not a feature. Building for one chain well is harder than building for 12 poorly.

**Fix:** Pick one chain. Show deep integration. Mention multi-chain as a future expansion, not a current capability.

## Trap 10: Numbers Without Sources

**What it looks like:** "The DeFi market will be $10T by 2030."

**Why it fails:** Where did this number come? If you can't cite it, the reader assumes you made it up.

**Fix:** Cite every number. "DeFi TVL is $85B today (source: DeFi Llama, March 2026)."

## Trap 11: The Solution Without a Problem

**What it looks like:** A beautiful product deck that never explains who hurts or why they care.

**Why it fails:** If there's no problem, there's no market. No market = no investment.

**Fix:** The problem slide is non-negotiable. Every deck must answer: who has pain, how much does it cost them, and why haven't they solved it?

## Trap 12: Deck Over Product

**What it looks like:** A polished 20-slide deck with no product to show.

**Why it fails:** In crypto, the product is the contract. If it's not deployed, it doesn't exist.

**Fix:** Build the thing first. A deck for a deployed contract is 10x more powerful than a deck for an idea.

## The Checklist

Before sending any deck, verify:

- [ ] Problem is specific and named (not "DeFi is hard")
- [ ] Solution is demonstrated (screenshot or live link)
- [ ] No buzzword-only slides
- [ ] Numbers are sourced
- [ ] Team has verifiable track record
- [ ] Ask is specific (amount + milestones)
- [ ] Token is not the lead story (if you have one)
- [ ] One chain, deep integration (not multi-chain theater)
- [ ] Architecture fits on one slide (4-5 boxes max)
- [ ] 12 slides or fewer (appendix is separate)
